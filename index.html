Overview

What This Study Does — and Does Not — Claim

Anchor Statement The CRS platform is promising not because it obviously beats ion clocks in absolute precision, but because it offers a differently sourced and potentially better modelled rank-2 perturbation for extracting quadrupole-sensitive ionic-core quantities.

This repository contains an open feasibility study exploring whether the circular Rydberg state (CRS) platform developed by the Meinert group at the 5. Physikalisches Institut, Universität Stuttgart, could be used for precision measurements of ionic-core atomic-structure quantities relevant to strontium ion clocks.

The core idea is that the electric-field gradient generated by a circular Rydberg electron at the position of the Sr⁺ ionic core provides a geometrically constrained perturbation that could be used to extract quadrupole moments, E2 matrix elements, and related quantities under electromagnetic boundary conditions qualitatively different from those of a Paul trap.

Scope Constraint This is not a claim of demonstrated precision, nor a programmatic commitment. It is a feasibility analysis that identifies candidate measurement targets, ranks them by plausibility, specifies falsifiable feasibility gates, and defines a no-go criterion. All quantitative estimates are order-of-magnitude. The study explicitly concludes that CRS is not a replacement for ion clocks but a potential differential probe of atomic structure under distinct conditions.

Constraint selection rule: CANONICAL-SUPPORTED — peer-reviewed literature as primary anchor; preprints admitted provisionally.

Strategic framing: Orthogonal systematics. Not a competition with ion clocks for absolute accuracy, but a differential probe of atomic structure under distinct electromagnetic boundary conditions.

§1 · Problem Definition

Which Targets, and Why CRS?

Question: Which atomic-structure targets relevant to ionic clocks could be measured or constrained using the divalent-atom circular Rydberg state (CRS) platform, and for which of these does the CRS environment offer a genuine advantage over conventional trapped-ion methods?

1.1 Target Classes

Quadrupole-sensitive quantities

Targets whose measurement depends on knowledge of or sensitivity to the electric-field gradient at the ionic core: electric quadrupole moments Θ(D₅/₂) and Θ(D₃/₂) of ⁸⁸Sr⁺, E2 transition matrix elements ⟨D‖Q‖S⟩, and the nuclear quadrupole moment Q(⁸⁷Sr) via hyperfine structure.

Tensor-shift quantities

Targets whose measurement is complicated by entanglement of tensor Stark shifts with the electric quadrupole shift in conventional traps: the differential tensor polarisability Δα₂ and individual Zeeman-sublevel differential shifts under controlled field gradients.

Scalar / BBR quantities

Targets where conventional ion clocks are already at or approaching the 10⁻¹⁹ level: Δα₀, BBR shift coefficient, and absolute clock transition frequency.

1.2 Exclusion

The scalar/BBR quantities are excluded from the primary target list. The current best measurement of Δα₀ for ⁸⁸Sr⁺ achieves 4.1 × 10⁻⁴ fractional uncertainty, and the PTB clock operates at 7.9 × 10⁻¹⁹ systematic uncertainty. Competing on these quantities requires Hz-level spectroscopy on the 674 nm transition inside the CRS — currently three orders of magnitude beyond demonstrated resolution. These quantities are listed under Category C.

§2 · Platform Mechanism

The Circular Rydberg Gradient

2.1 Core Idea

The circular Rydberg electron in state |n, ℓ = n−1, m = n−1⟩ generates a Coulomb electric field and field gradient at the position of the ionic core. Because the circular state has maximal angular momentum and a well-defined orbital geometry (a thin ring of radius ⟨r⟩ ≈ n²a₀), this field has a simpler geometric provenance than externally applied trap gradients:

Internally generated — not dependent on external electrode geometry, patch fields, or calibration drift. Geometrically constrained at leading order — the leading-order field gradient is determined by the ideal circular-state quantum numbers (n, ℓ, m) and fundamental constants. Orientation-locked — the gradient tensor orientation is set by m = n−1 relative to the quantisation axis.

The interaction Hamiltonian between the ionic core and the Rydberg electron's field can be represented schematically as:

Hint = −α₀|ERyd|² − α₂ T₂(ERyd) · T₂(D) + Θ · ∇ERyd + …

The Rydberg electron's field at the core position scales as |ERyd| ~ e/(n⁴a₀²) and the gradient as |∇ERyd| ~ e/(n⁶a₀³). Both decrease rapidly with n. For n = 79: |∇ERyd| is on the order of 10⁸ V/m², comfortably in the range needed for kHz-scale quadrupole shifts.

2.2 Non-Ideal Terms (Mandatory)

The field gradient is not "known" — it is "modelled with corrections." The following non-ideal contributions must be quantified in any metrological extraction:

  1. State impurity (coupled to item 2): Admixture of elliptical states (ℓ < n−1) changes the field-gradient tensor. Stray dc electric fields that induce ℓ-mixing simultaneously distort the spatial symmetry, producing a tensor perturbation that couples to the quadrupole interaction. State impurity and stray-field perturbations must be treated jointly.
  2. External stray electric fields (coupled to item 1): Couple to the CRS polarisability (scaling as n⁷) and shift the circular orbit off-axis. Residual fields at the ~mV/m level produce perturbations that grow with n.
  3. Cavity-induced modifications: ITO-coated capacitor plates provide microwave-frequency mode suppression (BBR suppression), but static and low-frequency field distortions are less constrained.
  4. Tweezer-induced perturbations: The trapping light field interacts with the Rydberg electron (ponderomotive effect, growing with n) and with the ionic core (ac Stark shift, characterised).
  5. Wavefunction averaging: The naïve point-charge model must be replaced by ⟨n,ℓ,m|∇E|n,ℓ,m⟩. Corrections from finite orbital width are non-negligible at the required precision.
  6. Relativistic and QED corrections: At the ~10⁻⁴ level, relevant and calculable.

3.2 Category A1 — Direct, High-Potential

#1: Θ(D₃/₂) — Lowest-hanging fruit. Builds on Wirth et al. (2024). kHz-scale differential shift already observed. No direct experimental measurement exists. Even ~10% extraction would be a new result.

#2: Θ(D₅/₂) — Most clock-relevant. Current best: 12% uncertainty (Barwood 2004), limited by external gradient calibration. CRS replaces this with forward-model uncertainty. Signal ~5–10 kHz at n = 79. D₅/₂ coupling inside CRS not yet demonstrated (requires Step 2).

3.3 Category A2 — High-Potential, Model-Dependent

#3: E2 matrix elements — ∇ERyd drives E2 transitions; Wirth et al. shows coherent driving on all eight D₃/₂ Zeeman components. Extraction requires comparing Rabi frequency with calculated gradient — longer inference chain. More model-dependent.

3.4 Category B

#4: Δα₂ — PTB explicitly cannot separate EQS from tensor Stark in Paul trap. CRS geometry fixes the E/∇E ratio, making them separable in principle. Requires ~100 Hz resolution and validated Θ. Category B now, potentially A2 at improved resolution.

#5: Zeeman-sublevel shifts — CRS probes individual mJ responses without the averaging that clock cancellation schemes impose. Useful for testing atomic-structure calculations.

3.5 Category B-Deferred

#6: Q(⁸⁷Sr) — Long inference chain: CRS → E2 → qJ → Q. Recent ~10% theory discrepancy. Requires ⁸⁷Sr CRS (speculative). Retained for completeness, deferred.

§6 · Discriminant Conditions

Feasibility Gates

These are conjunctive: all must be satisfied for full metrological viability.

DC-F1 · CRITICAL
Core spectroscopy resolution ≤ 1 kHz
Technical-noise-limited (D₅/₂ Fourier limit is ~0.4 Hz). Single most critical gate. Plausible within 12–24 months given demonstrated spin-echo coherence of ~ms; requires no new principle but substantial technical work.
DC-F2 · CRITICAL
CRS purity ≥ 99% (ℓ-mixing < 1%)
Quantitative characterisation of circular-state preparation fidelity; purity high enough that admixture-induced gradient-tensor uncertainty remains below 1% of δνQ. Exact threshold depends on the forward model's mapping from admixture fraction to tensor perturbation magnitude.
DC-F3 · STANDARD
Stray-field-bounded gradient uncertainty < 1% of δνQ
At n = 79, δνQ ≈ 5 kHz → stray-field shifts must be bounded below ~50 Hz. Includes both direct field perturbation and indirect ℓ-mixing perturbation. Plausible with standard compensation techniques plus Stark-spectroscopy calibration.
DC-F4 · DECISIVE
n-consistency ≤ 1%
Θ extracted at n = 79 and n = 89 must agree within mutual uncertainties. Decisive physics test. Working expectation: 2–3 years.
DC-F5 · FINAL
Cross-comparison with conventional value
CRS extraction compared with conventional value at informative precision. Working expectation: 3–5 years.
No-Go Criterion If the n-consistency check (DC-F4) fails at the > 5% level after inclusion of all identified correction terms from §2.2, the programme should not be framed as precision extraction of atomic constants. It should revert to qualitative cross-check or model-validation status. This is the hard stop condition.

Three Steps to Extraction

D₅/₂ Extension + n-Consistency

Cross-Compare + Advanced Targets

§8 · Failure Modes

What Could Go Wrong

8.1 Forward-model breakdown

n-consistency check fails → model corrections dominate. Mitigate: ≥ 3 n-values; exploit distinct n-scaling of corrections.

8.2 Insufficient resolution

674 nm laser cannot reach sub-kHz in CRS apparatus. Mitigate: clock-lab technology transfer; spin echo.

8.3 Purity ceiling

ℓ-mixing saturates at ~5% due to stray-field coupling (§2.2 items 1–2). Purity and field problems cannot be solved independently. Mitigate: joint purity + field diagnostics; improved circularisation.

8.4 Decoherence at signal-optimal n

T₂ too short at n ≈ 79–89. Mitigate: field compensation; slightly higher n (trade signal for coherence).

§9 · Strategic Positioning

Differential Probe, Not Replacement

Core Thesis CRS is not a replacement for ion clocks. It is a differential probe of atomic structure under distinct electromagnetic boundary conditions.

The core ion inside a CRS sees no Paul-trap micromotion, no ion-ion Coulomb coupling, an internally generated field gradient, and a cavity-modified BBR environment. Agreement or disagreement with ion-clock values is scientifically informative — but only if the CRS measurement itself is interpretable, i.e. only if DC-F2 (purity), DC-F3 (field bounds), and DC-F4 (model validation) are satisfied.

A discrepancy arising from unmodelled ℓ-mixing rather than genuine Θ physics would be noise, not signal. The "either way informative" claim is conditional on the feasibility gates.

Publication path: reproduce → extract → compare (§7).

What Remains Open

10.1 Pending

Forward model at n = 79 with all §2.2 corrections, including stray-field/ℓ-mixing coupling — the true bottleneck. Also pending: n-dependence study for model validation, ⁸⁷Sr CRS preparation (speculative), and frequency comb integration.

10.2 Load-Bearing Assumptions

CRS purity quantifiable at < 1% admixture level. Forward model validatable across n (DC-F4). Sub-kHz ionic-core spectroscopy achievable (technology transfer, not new physics). External field perturbations boundable below model uncertainty. Stray-field / ℓ-mixing coupling can be modelled or measured independently — if not, the "self-referenced" advantage is lost.

10.3 Next Technical Task: Forward-Model Scaffold

The immediate bottleneck is the forward-model derivation, requiring four layers:

Layer 1 — Ideal circular-state gradient

Write ⟨n, n−1, n−1|∇E|n, n−1, n−1⟩ in irreducible tensor form using hydrogen-like wavefunctions with quantum-defect corrections for Sr. Identify the exact rank-2 tensor structure.

Layer 2 — Finite-width wavefunction correction

Replace the point-charge gradient with the full expectation value over the circular-state radial probability distribution. Estimate fractional correction relative to leading order as a function of n.

Layer 3 — Stray-field / ℓ-mixing coupled correction

For a given |Estray|, calculate the admixture amplitude of ℓ = n−2 and ℓ = n−3 states via Stark mixing. Compute the tensor perturbation to ∇ERyd. Express Δ(∇E)/∇E as a function of |Estray| and n.

Layer 4 — Observable-to-target inversion for Θ(D₃/₂)

Define the explicit formula: Θ = measured δνQ × h / (calculated ∇ERyd), and propagate uncertainties from spectroscopic noise, forward-model uncertainty, and purity bounds.

§12 · Tutorial

Reading This Dossier as a PhD Student

12.1 What Is a Circular Rydberg State?

An atom in a Rydberg state has its outermost electron excited to a very high energy level, characterised by a large principal quantum number n (here, n ≈ 79–103). In a "circular" Rydberg state, the electron also has the maximum possible orbital angular momentum: ℓ = n − 1, m = n − 1. Classically, this corresponds to the electron orbiting the nucleus in a thin ring. The orbital radius scales as n²a₀, so at n = 79 the electron orbits at roughly 0.3 μm from the nucleus — macroscopic by atomic standards.

Circular states are long-lived because radiative decay pathways are highly constrained by angular-momentum selection rules. This makes blackbody-radiation-induced transitions the dominant loss mechanism, which can be suppressed by placing the atom inside a microwave cavity. The Stuttgart group uses ITO-coated glass plates separated by ~1 cm, achieving lifetimes exceeding 10 ms at room temperature.

12.2 Why Strontium? The Divalent Advantage

Most Rydberg experiments use alkali atoms (Rb, Cs), which have a single valence electron. When excited to a Rydberg state, there is no remaining optical electron. Strontium is divalent: it has two valence electrons. When one is excited to a circular Rydberg state, the other remains bound to the Sr²⁺ core, forming an Sr⁺-like ion. This ionic core retains its own electronic structure — including the S₁/₂, P₁/₂, D₃/₂, and D₅/₂ states familiar from Sr⁺ ion-trap experiments — and can be addressed with standard laser wavelengths even while the outer electron is in the circular Rydberg state.

12.3 The Electric Quadrupole Shift

An optical atomic clock measures the frequency of a specific electronic transition. For ⁸⁸Sr⁺ ion clocks, the reference is S₁/₂ → D₅/₂ at 674 nm (~445 THz). The D₅/₂ state has a non-spherical charge distribution (a "quadrupole moment" Θ). When this sits in an electric-field gradient ∇E, the state energy shifts proportionally to Θ × ∇E. In a Paul trap the gradient comes from trap electrodes; in a CRS, from the circular Rydberg electron.

The EQS is one of the largest systematic shifts in Sr⁺ clocks. Clock groups cancel it by averaging over Zeeman sublevels (Dubé et al.), which eliminates the shift but also destroys information about the quadrupole moment itself. The current best measurement of Θ(D₅/₂) has only 12% precision because calibrating the external field gradient is difficult.

12.4 The Core Idea

Can we use the circular Rydberg electron's electric-field gradient as a "calibrated" source for measuring the ionic core's quadrupole moment? The argument: the circular Rydberg electron orbits at a known radius in a known geometry, generating a calculable gradient. By measuring the quadrupole shift of the ionic core's D-state levels inside the CRS and comparing with the calculated gradient, one can extract Θ. The gradient has a simpler geometric origin than a Paul-trap gradient, but it is not truly "known" — it must be modelled with corrections.

12.5 The A/B/C Classification

Three questions for each target: (1) Is the CRS gradient the right kind of perturbation? (2) Is the CRS measurement likely to be better than conventional methods? (3) Is the inferential chain short enough to be trustworthy? Targets scoring well on all three are A1; those requiring a validated forward model are A2; independent cross-checks only are B; clearly inferior to conventional methods are C.

12.6 The Forward Model

The theoretical machinery that converts a measured spectroscopic shift into an extracted atomic constant. It must: (1) calculate the ideal gradient, (2) add finite-width, relativistic, and QED corrections, (3) model ℓ-mixing from stray fields and other non-ideal effects, and (4) propagate all uncertainties. The decisive test is the n-consistency check: if the same Θ is extracted at two different n values and results agree, the model is validated.

12.7 How to Read the Feasibility Gates

DC-F1 (resolution) and DC-F2 (purity) are apparatus diagnostics. DC-F3 (field bounds) requires careful characterisation. DC-F4 (n-consistency) is the decisive physics test. DC-F5 (cross-comparison) is the scientific payoff. The no-go criterion says: if DC-F4 fails badly (> 5%), the programme should not claim precision extraction.
